AMENDMENT XIII

Overview Timeline Impact Quick Facts Sources Test

Section 1:

"Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction."

Section 2:

"Slavery and Involuntary servitude is banned. People who commited crimes and are in jail are subject to be slaves. Congress is allowed to reinforce this law with proper collective vote."

To Over Simplify it

Slavery and Involuntary servitude were deemed unconstitutional; unless you have committed any crimes. This meant anyone in jail was subjected to become a slave.

This Amendment also allowed Congress to reinforce this law whenever they saw fit, of course only with the proper collective votes.

General information:

  • Although many of the founders acknowledged slavery as wrong, they contributed to the continuation of it.

  • Two different cultural beliefs split the country. With the south depending economically on slaves and the north depending on factories. They both began to mistrust each other since they both had different ideas.

Jim Crow Laws:

  • Jim Crow Laws were a set of laws that allowed states to legalized racial segregation

  • These laws existed from 1877 to 1968

  • Those who defied them could face anything from fines, to Jail sentences, and possibly death

  • Examples of these laws ranged from making "Colored People being forced to drink out of segregated water coolers" to even "it not being required for white doctors to nurse black men"

The Impact of the 13th Amendment

This amendment has impacted the world greatly. The biggest thing this was responsible for, if you don't know already, is that it abolished slavery; and since most slaves were being used for their labor, the nation's economy took a stabbing. Most factories and plantations relied on Slaves.

While it neutralized the conflict going between the north and south, the introduction of Jim Crow laws only emphasizes the South's reliance on blacks. Segregation was stronger than ever now that certain things such as bathrooms, schools, etc were being separated by color. However, with the Jim Crow Laws being outlawed in 1964 people began to accept one another for who they are. But despite it, we do still have large pockets of racism sprinkled throughout the country.

It could be said that this Amendment was somewhat a success since it did accomplish its goal of eliminating slavery, but racism still remained for many years after.
